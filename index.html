Design of a Testing Setup for Diffusion Coefficient Measurement

Naomie Curis Friedman, Nicolas Courion, Ari Kaufman, Simon Crevier

Abstract

The goal of this project is to assist the Graduate Biomechanics Laboratory team at McGill University in improving the performance of drug-eluting stents. Specifically, the experimental setup must allow for the measurement of the diffusion coefficient of the compound, which is crucial for selecting the right compound and improving stent design. To achieve this goal, a Diaphragm Cell method was chosen as an efficient way to measure the diffusion coefficient. The method involves two containers with respective volumes, separated by a porous membrane. The donor side of the membrane is filled with a simulated medical compound, and the receiver container is filled with a buffer, typically water. Both solutions are stirred and allowed to interact, and the diffusion process is measured over time. The diffusion coefficient is then calculated based on concentrations which are sampled at a constant interval. The key concern in this design is to reduce leakage. Three designs were created, and the chosen design was then iterated upon numerous times to reduce leakage. Our final iteration experienced no leakage in the entirety of the experiment, and it will continually be developed for future research.
